# Hot-foil vs cold foil — cost, finish, and when each is the right answer

By Sonia Sun, Founder, Huamei 華美 — since 1992. Updated 8 May 2026.

A buyer briefing a metallic mark on a luxury packaging project is choosing between two techniques that both leave gold on paper but otherwise have almost nothing in common. Hot-foil uses heat, pressure, and a metal die. Cold foil uses ink-station adhesive, in-line with offset printing, with no heat at all. The two have different tooling costs, different registration tolerances, and different right answers for different jobs. This page is the working comparison.

## What each one is, in one paragraph

> Hot-foil presses a heated die against a foil ribbon to bond foil onto paper, holding ±0.1 mm registration. Cold foil applies foil in-line with offset printing using a UV-cured adhesive, with ~±0.3 mm registration. Hot-foil suits sharp small marks; cold foil suits large flooded areas.

**Hot-foil** is the older technique. A metal die is heated to between 120 and 160 °C, brought down on a foil ribbon laid over paper, held for four-tenths to eight-tenths of a second, and lifted. The foil bonds to the paper at the die-pressed area. Tooling is a custom die per project.

**Cold foil** is the newer technique. The first offset station on the press lays a UV-cured adhesive in the shape of the desired foil mark. The foil ribbon is then pressed onto the still-wet adhesive, and the foil bonds where adhesive was deposited. Tooling is a printing plate per project — cheaper and faster to produce than a die.

## Cost shape

The two techniques have different cost curves and they cross at a different volume than most buyers assume.

**Hot-foil tooling.** A magnesium or copper die runs $80–250 per project depending on detail. The die lasts 50,000+ impressions. Per-piece foil cost is low because the foil ribbon advances only across the die, not the whole sheet. **Best at scale: hot-foil's per-piece cost falls quickly past 1,000 pieces.**

**Cold foil tooling.** A standard offset plate, integrated with the colour pass. No die. Per-piece foil cost is higher because the foil ribbon advances across the entire sheet. The trade-off: setup time is part of the offset run, not separate.

For a 200-piece luxury launch with a small foil mark, **hot-foil is roughly 30% cheaper than cold foil** because the die cost amortizes across enough pieces to beat the per-sheet adhesive cost. For a 5,000-piece run with foil flooding 30% of the sheet, **cold foil is roughly 40% cheaper** because the die would have to be re-inked many times and the per-piece foil cost wins.

## Edge sharpness — the visible difference

Stand a hot-foiled mark next to a cold-foiled mark at one foot. The difference is the edge.

Hot-foil produces a sharp edge because the die pressure cuts the foil cleanly along the die geometry. Letter terminals close, fine lines stay open. **±0.1 mm registration is held by the press operator with a die-aligning tool every 200 sheets.**

Cold foil produces a softer edge because the adhesive spreads slightly under foil pressure during transfer. ±0.3 mm registration is the realistic tolerance. Letter terminals on small type close up; fine lines below 0.4 mm fail.

For any logo, mark, or accent that has to read crisp at three feet, hot-foil is the answer. For any large flooded area where edge sharpness is not the point — a metallic background, an accent panel, a band across a wraparound shopper — cold foil is the answer.

## Substrate compatibility

Cold foil is choosier about substrate. The UV-cured adhesive needs a non-porous surface to set. Coated art papers (silk, matte, gloss) accept cold foil cleanly. Uncoated stocks (Gmund, Fedrigoni) absorb the adhesive unevenly, and the foil bonds inconsistently across the sheet.

Hot-foil is substrate-agnostic. The press operator dials temperature and dwell to the stock, and a properly-tuned hot-foil pass works on uncoated paper, coated art paper, book-cloth, recycled stocks, and even some specialty substrates that other techniques cannot touch. The trade-off is calibration time per substrate, which hot-foil pricing already includes.

This single fact — substrate flexibility — is why most of the seventeen foils on Huamei's cabinet are run through the hot-foil press, not the cold-foil station.

## What about digital foil?

A third technique exists for completeness. **Digital foil** (Scodix, MGI, similar systems) lays a clear UV-cured adhesive in arbitrary shapes — including variable data, different on every piece — and presses foil onto it. Registration is roughly ±0.5 mm. Per-piece cost is the highest of the three.

Use digital foil only when the foil mark genuinely changes piece-to-piece (personalized names, individual SKUs, limited-edition sequential numbering). For any project where every piece has the same foil mark, hot-foil or cold-foil is the answer.

## When each is right — the buyer's rule

**Use hot-foil when:**
- The mark is small and has to read sharp (logos, signatures, accent letters)
- The substrate is uncoated, book-cloth, or specialty
- The volume is under ~2,000 pieces
- The project includes [registered emboss-and-foil](/craft/emboss) — only hot-foil holds the ±0.1 mm tolerance for the dual-pass alignment

**Use cold foil when:**
- The mark is large and flooded (background panels, full-bleed metallics, accent bands)
- The substrate is coated art paper
- The volume is over ~3,000 pieces
- The project is running on offset anyway and adding a foil station is cheaper than a separate hot-foil pass

For projects in the 2,000–3,000 piece range, the call comes down to substrate and edge requirements. We will spec both options against the brief and post a sample of each.

## How Huamei specs it

Three sentences on a brief is enough:

> "Foil mark sharpness: critical." → hot-foil
> "Foil flooding > 30% of sheet area." → cold foil
> "Variable / per-piece different." → digital foil

We sample both hot-foil and cold-foil on the actual project stock during the 7–10 day sample round. The brand sample team confirms the edge sharpness at one foot — by hand, not from a screen — before the production run is scheduled.
