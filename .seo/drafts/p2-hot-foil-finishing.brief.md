## Outline

### Intro (60–90 words)

- One-sentence definitive answer: Hot-foil stamping is the technique that pulls metallic, pearlescent, or pigmented foil onto paper or board under heat and pressure, leaving a reflective mark that ink alone cannot produce.
- One sentence anchoring to Huamei: Seventeen foils on file in-house, run on Heidelberg and KBA offset presses across four factories — for cosmetic launches (Lancôme, Estée Lauder, L'Oréal Paris), spirits gift packs (Wuliangye, Yangshao, Dukang), and seasonal ranges since 1992.

### H2 — What hot-foil stamping is, in one paragraph (featured-snippet target)

**30–40 word answer block (immediately under H2):**

> Hot-foil stamping presses a heated metal die against a foil ribbon laid on paper or board. The foil's release layer separates from its carrier and bonds to the substrate, leaving a reflective image. Press temperature 120–160 °C; dwell 0.4–0.8 s; registration tolerance ±0.1 mm.

Then 2–3 supporting paragraphs:
- The three variables (temperature, dwell, pressure) and what each one does
- Why the technique survives the box's full life (foil is mechanically bonded, not surface-printed)
- Why ink alone can't replace it — anything reading as *metallic* in luxury packaging is foiled, not printed.

### H2 — Hot-foil vs cold foil vs digital foil (~180 words)

A buyer-side comparison. Three columns:

| Technique | Ideal use | Registration | Cost shape |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hot-foil | Sharp small marks, spot accents, edges | ±0.1 mm | Tooling: dies. Per-piece: low. Best at scale. |
| Cold foil | Large flooded areas, in-line with offset | ~±0.3 mm | No die tooling; plate-based. Cheaper at low volume but visibly softer edge. |
| Digital foil (Scodix-style) | Variable / personalized | ~±0.5 mm | Per-piece higher. Use only when each piece is different. |

Bottom line: hot-foil is the right answer for any logo, mark, or accent that has to read crisp at three feet. Cold foil works for backgrounds. Digital foil is for variable data only.

### H2 — Pairing foil to substrate (the part most pages skip) (~180 words)

This is where Huamei's experience shows.

- **Uncoated paper (Gmund, Fedrigoni)**: foil reads as embedded — the substrate's tooth lets the foil sit slightly proud. Cool metallics work especially well; warm metallics need a slightly higher dwell.
- **Coated art papers (silk, matte, gloss)**: foil sits flat and reads as printed. Use for clean, modern marks; avoid pigmented foils on silk because they appear waxy.
- **Book-cloth (Iris, Wibalin)**: hot-foil on cloth requires a softer die (the cloth weave compresses unevenly). Pearl and warm metallics work; pigmented foils struggle.
- **Recycled / alt-fibre**: foil bonds unpredictably; we always proof these. Some recycled stocks reject foil entirely on the first pass and require a primer coat.

Tie back to the three variables: substrate choice changes the *temperature* and *dwell* the press operator dials in.

### H2 — Registered emboss-and-foil — the house specialty (~180 words)

A featured-snippet candidate. Definition + tolerance + how to spec it.

- **Definition:** Registered emboss-and-foil is a single mark where the embossed die rises *and* the foil lights at the same point, in perfect alignment.
- **Tolerance:** Held to ±0.1 mm at Huamei. (Industry typical: ±0.3 mm.)
- **Why it's hard:** Two passes have to land on the same coordinate. Substrate moves under heat. Die wear adds drift over a 5,000-piece run.
- **How to spec it:** Provide a single dieline showing the emboss outline AND the foil mark in one layer. Don't separate them. Specify foil colour and emboss type (sculpted vs flat).
- **House example to cite:** Lancôme Love (heart-shape rigid + foil), L'Oréal Gem (faceted gold gem). Both visible on `/volumes/lancome-love` and `/volumes/loreal-gem`.

### H2 — When hot-foil isn't the answer (~120 words)

Honest counter-position. Hot-foil is wrong for:
- **Photographic gradients** — foil can't render mid-tones; use printed metallic ink instead.
- **Very small fonts under 6pt** — foil clog. Use embossed-only or printed.
- **Edge-to-edge metallic surfaces over 100 cm²** — use metallized paper laminate; hot-foil at that size is unstable.
- **Single-piece prototypes (<50 pcs)** — die tooling cost dominates. Use a digital foil press for the prototype, hot-foil for the production run.

This paragraph is the page's credibility moment — naming when the technique doesn't fit shows we know the tool.
